Doris
Team Members:
  • Richard Belovic - Programmer
  • Angus Jardine - Artist
Doris is the first project Angus and I decided to make. We needed experience before we worked on anything larger scale, which is why we created a short top-down RPG where the player defeated monsters and made their way to the final boss.
Doris features a modular armour system, inventory, shop, and enemies would drop randomised bags of items depending on their loot tables. Each item had its own stats and we created multiple armour sets that the player could find. The player could sell items at the traders and buy potions or other items to help them on their journey. The inventory/shop and modular armour systems were by far the most difficult to create, especially as they all had to be able to interact with each other. Although the game was quite buggy and the combat felt sluggish, we felt very satsified with the game as our first project and we ended up learning a lot. The final boss arena was similar to Kitava in Path of Exile or Belial from Diablo 3. The boss would hit the ground, releasing projectiles that had to be dodged and occasionally making boulders fall while spewing vomit. After the boss was defeated, the game would end.
6 Bodies
Team Members:
  • Richard Belovic - Programmer
  • Angus Jardine - Artist
6 Bodies is the third game Angus and I made. After finishing Johnny's Deathwish, we wanted to get experience creating a 3D experience - we decided to make a short horror game demo. itch.io page
The game starts with a cutscene of the player driving to a gas station with a radio playing in the background. Once the player exits the car, they can explore the surroundings, keep listening to the radio, or just fill up their car and head inside the gas station. 6 Bodies' main antagonist is a 2D sprite portraying a tall creepy man. The player ends up getting locked inside the gas station, and has to make their way into the basemant where the main part of the game takes place. The large basement is used as a hunting ground for the AI, if the player gets caught, they die and return back to the beginning of the basement. The player has to sneak their way around the basement, looking for keys and ways through while dodging encounters with the hunter AI. The main objective is to open a big metal door at one of the basement and reach it, after which the demo ends. We went for the Daggerfall approach of portraying the creepy man, making sprites of multiple angles and switching them based on where the AI was looking relative to the player. The AI has the following behaviours:
  • Patrolling by picking random spots in the basement to walk to
  • Greatly speeds up when the player is unable to see him, this increases the potential for jump scare moments and means that the player feels less safe
  • Begins hunting the player if he seems him within his range or can hear the footsteps (depending on if the player is walking/crouching/sprinting)
  • Loses the player if he doesn't see him for a duration of time

Johnny's Deathwish
Team Members:
  • Richard Belovic - Programmer
  • Angus Jardine - Artist
After completing Doris, Angus and I were set on making a larger and more polished project. We decided on making Johnny's Deathwish, a challenging 2D sidescroller. itch.io page The goal of this project was to create our own polished 2D character controller that we could throw into a bunch of levels with a variety of traps.
Instead of waiting to finish the game, we were sending builds to our friends almost every week from the start, in order to get feedback on the feel of the character and the difficulty of the levels. This helped us find bugs that we were previously unaware of, which was a massive improvement over our first project - Doris.
The biggest challenge in the making of this project was the creation of the character controller as it needed to be able slide down and climb between walls, with many different potential edge-case scenarios that could break it. Angus and I worked on a large array of traps:
  • Boulders
  • Spikes
  • Saws
  • Timed Lasers
  • Turrets
  • Swinging blades
We used these to create a variety of challenges for the player to overcome, and to avoid overuse of the same traps in different levels. The later levels feature 'Builder' AI, they're capable of jumping over pitfalls and climbing walls in order to hunt the player down. These were by far the most fun to program and test. A total of 4 regions and 27 levels were made, the final level of the game features a boss fight against the man that stole Johnny's ducky. The boss has three phases: rotating around the arena and spewing lasers + projectiles, charging the player horizontally, and finally, going invulnerable while spawning two flying AI.
Heroic Dregs
Team Members:
  • Richard Belovic - Programmer
  • Angus Jardine - Artist
In our latest project, Angus and I decided we wanted to really focus on creating a polished top-down 2D experience. Vampire Survivors and other similar horde games have a fairly straight forward and satisfying gameplay loop: kill monsters -> game gets harder -> upgrade your character -> kill more monsters. We decided that this simple gameplay loop was a perfect starting point for building upon and creating a fun, polished and replayable gameplay demo, with the possibility of being able to expand it in the future.

The game is still in development but all the main systems and goals have been completed, here's a quick look at our ability system.



The player starts off with two main abilities (for example, the roll and arrow shot). They can upgrade their character every time they level up to get:
  • Passive abilities - these include, fireballs that shoot out and explode, bombs that drop on the ground or even knives that circle around the player
  • Active ability modifiers - augments to the active abilties that can be used to set enemies alight or make them spawn poison ground once they die
  • Runes - these are stackable modifiers that affect all your abilities. More projectiles, critical strike chance, projectile speed, attack speed etc.

We were struggling a lot with performance, especially having more than 400 enemies on screen. I spent some time learning the Unity Data Oriented Technology Stack and converted all our projectiles/attacks and enemies to be entities for massive performance gains. This involved rebuilding our enemies and projectiles from ground up to to be controlled by a number of custom DOTS systems and jobs, most of which are now parallelised.
